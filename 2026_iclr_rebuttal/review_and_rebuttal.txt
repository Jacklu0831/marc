# Reviewer 1

Summary:
This paper proposes Context Tuning, a method that improves few shot adaptation (with additional parameters) of LLMs without updating model weights. The core idea is to initializing existing prompt tuning and prefix tuning method with embeddings obtained directly from task specific demonstration examples. As a result, the paper introduces two variants: CT Prompt tunes a soft prompt initialized from the demonstrations. CT KV initializes and tunes layerwise key value prefixes extracted from the model activations on the same demonstrations. The authors further introduce leave one out masking to prevent target leakage during training, and token dropout to reduce overfitting. Across multiple benchmarks, Context Tuning outperforms standard prompt and prefix tuning and is competitive with test time training while being substantially more efficient, especially for CT KV.

Soundness: 2: fair
Presentation: 2: fair
Contribution: 1: poor
Strengths:
Parameter efficiency and stability. The method keeps model weights frozen and only learns a small set of prompt or prefix parameters, which preserves the base model while enabling task adaptation.

Consistent gains in few shot settings. Experiments show improvements over vanilla in context learning and over standard prompt or prefix tuning on several benchmarks. The gains are robust across a range of demonstration counts and across different base model sizes. But, much of the empirical benefit appears to come from a stronger initialization of existing prompt or prefix tuning, rather than a fundamentally new optimization mechanism. I list this more fully under weaknesses.

Weaknesses:
Limited novelty in mechanism. The main algorithmic move is to initialize the trainable prompt or prefix from demonstration embeddings, then optimize as in standard prompt or prefix tuning. This is a strong and practical idea, but conceptually close to prior methods and may be seen as an improved initialization strategy rather than a new optimization principle.

Positioning relative to test time training. The paper reports results for TTT plus CT KV and presents this combined setting among the proposed methods. Since TTT is an existing and separate approach that updates model weights, it is not clear that TTT plus CT KV should be considered an integral contribution of this paper. A clearer separation of baselines, proposed methods, and combinations would help, along with ablations that isolate where the gains come from.

Interpretation of robustness to label noise. The paper highlights that CT KV remains strong even when many demonstration labels are corrupted. However, in an in context optimization view, a method should ideally adjust its behavior when labels change. If performance is largely unchanged under heavy label corruption, this can suggest that the method relies more on input side regularities or priors than on learning from the provided labels. This reduces the strength of the robustness claim as evidence of learning from context.

Missing specification details. Some core definitions and operations are underspecified. In leave one out masking, the construction of P^{\text{minus i}}_{\text{CT}} for prompts and of the corresponding context representation for prefixes is not formally detailed. The paper says the relevant tokens are masked out from the attention view, but it does not specify whether this is implemented by zeroing keys and values, by blocking attention through an attention mask, or by removing those prefix positions from the cache. A precise definition would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Questions:
Variable length to fixed size initialization. Demonstration contexts can vary in length. How do you map variable length demonstrations to a fixed number of trainable prompt tokens or to a fixed per layer prefix budget. For CT Prompt, do you truncate or pool embeddings when the demonstration context exceeds the prompt length. For CT KV, how are per layer prefix lengths chosen and how are multiple demonstrations combined or allocated across that budget.

# Response to Reviewer 1

We thank the reviewer for the thoughtful and detailed feedback. We are encouraged that the reviewer recognizes our method’s consistent gains over strong baselines while keeping model weights frozen. We thoroughly address the concerns below and have updated the PDF accordingly.

Limited novelty in mechanism.

The reviewer correctly pointed out that Context Tuning involves an improved initialization strategy. However, we motivate and design two changes, Leave-One-Out Masking and Token Dropout, to the optimization loop in Section 4.3. In Table 3, we present an ablation study over all datasets that demonstrates the importance of both design decisions to Context Tuning’s performance. Without them, a naive use of the new initialization strategy (the “Neither” row in Table 3) performs even worse than Prefix Tuning on NLP-LR and BBH (Table 1). Therefore, Leave-One-Out Masking and Token Dropout are not auxiliary tricks added to squeeze out extra gains; they are core components that make Context Tuning effective.

Positioning relative to test time training… A clearer separation of baselines, proposed methods, and combinations would help…

We agree that TTT is a separate existing method and point this out in Section 4.2, where we also formalize TTT as an instance of our In-Context Optimization framework. In Table 1, we ablate the performance of TTT (under “Baselines”), CT-KV, and TTT+CT-KV separately over all datasets, matching the reviewer’s request. In the revised PDF, we made a minor change by putting TTT+CT-KV under “Combined Methods” to avoid confusion.

This ablation reveals two insights:

CT-KV matches TTT’s accuracy at a fraction of optimization time. We additionally derive the time complexity for both methods and show that CT-KV is more efficient because it does not recompute the KV-cache corresponding to the demonstration examples, while TTT does so for each training iteration.
TTT and CT-KV are complementary. TTT+CT-KV achieves higher performance than when TTT or CT-KV is used separately, indicating that optimizing weights (TTT) and context (CT-KV) can be effectively combined to achieve the best performance.
Interpretation of robustness to label noise.

We agree that a method should adjust its behavior when labels change, and observe it in various experiments: in Figure 4b, CT-KV’s accuracy indeed decreases at higher corruption rates, showing that it uses label semantics rather than ignoring them. Moreover, Figure 4a shows that CT-KV benefits strongly from far more demonstration pairs than the number of unique multiple-choice labels. If CT-KV relied primarily on input-side regularities or priors, these effects would not appear consistently across datasets.

Our robustness experiment targets a complementary question: does the method catastrophically fail once some labels are corrupted? Figure 4b shows that up to 75% corruption, CT-KV still outperforms Prefix Tuning and ICL, indicating that it learns a robust task representation under imperfect demonstration quality, an important property when real-world demonstration examples may include inconsistencies or user errors.

# Reviewer 3

Summary:
This paper introduces context tuning, a variant of existing continuous (i.e., parametric) prompt optimization approaches. Unlike previous methods that typically initialize parameters randomly, the proposed approach initializes them using demonstration representations. For training, it also incorporates additional techniques such as leave-one-out masking and token dropout, which resemble those used in the previous work called Test-Time Training (TTT). When evaluated on several benchmarks, including NLP-LR, MMLU, BBH, and ARC, the proposed method achieves performance comparable to existing methods and further improves results when combined with TTT.

While the paper introduces a new paradigm termed in-context optimization, encompassing both the proposed method (i.e., context tuning) and TTT, it remains unclear whether this terminology provides genuine conceptual novelty or clarity. The idea can arguably be well captured by existing notions such as context engineering, prompt tuning, or test-time compute scaling.

# Response to Reviewer 3

Comment:
We thank the reviewer for their thoughtful feedback. We are encouraged that our work is considered well written, aligned with related work, and supported by comprehensive experiments. We address the concerns point by point.

I appreciate the simplicity of the proposed idea, but it seems somewhat too incremental…

We appreciate the reviewer’s recognition of the simplicity of Context Tuning, and believe that simplicity paired with consistent improvements makes it a practical adaptation strategy.

Context Tuning is substantially different from TTT in mechanism and efficiency. While both fall under In-Context Optimization, TTT repeatedly reorders demonstration pairs, applies leave-one-out over token sequences, and updates LoRA parameters, requiring the model to reconstruct the task representation many times. In contrast, CT-KV extracts a structured KV representation from demonstrations once and directly optimizes this persistent representation, with Leave-One-Out Masking applied at KV segments rather than token sequences. This yields distinct optimization dynamics and the provable efficiency gains: Appendix B shows CT-KV’s training time scales linearly in demonstration counts, while TTT scales quadratically. Empirically (Table 1), CT-KV requires far less training time than TTT while it achieves competitive or better performance. These distinctions in mechanism and efficiency make Context Tuning meaningfully more than an incremental variant of TTT.

There is no clear analysis of why context tuning achieves further gains compared to foundational approaches…

We appreciate the interest in deeper clarity on Context Tuning’s gains. In particular, Table 3 cleanly decomposes CT-KV’s gains over Prefix Tuning. Section 5.7 interprets these results, which we summarize into a row-by-row format:

Neither. This initialization-only variant resembles Prefix Tuning but replaces random vectors with demonstration-derived ones. It performs similarly to Prefix Tuning (Table 1), confirming that initialization alone is insufficient because the model retrieves targets in its initialized context.
No Leave-One-Out Masking. Adding Token Dropout provides mild regularization and marginal gains.
No Token Dropout. Leave-One-Out Masking alone yields a large improvement by preventing retrieval of target outputs and forcing generalization from other examples.
Both. Using both mechanisms yields the highest performance on NLP-LR, MMLU, and BBH (with ARC discussed in Section 5.7).
The idea can arguably be well captured by existing notions such as context engineering, prompt tuning, or test-time compute scaling.

More fundamentally, it is intriguing that the trade-off inherent in these test-time training–like approaches…

We thank the reviewer for these broader questions on the positioning of test-time optimization relative to ICL. While ICL enables inference-time adaptation without further finetuning, we claim that ICO methods like TTT and Context Tuning introduce stronger task-specific adaptation. This is precisely why our evaluation includes both ICL and traditional finetuning baselines (Prompt/Prefix Tuning, LoRA variants), with Context Tuning showing significant gains over all of them (Table 1).

Conceptually, we view ICO as bridging ICL and finetuning. Context Tuning leverages the LLM’s inherent ICL ability by initializing from demonstrations, while gradient-based optimization moves beyond superficial demonstration patterns [1]. This hybrid approach preserves the portability and safety of frozen-weight models while surpassing both ICL and finetuning baselines across benchmarks.

Task-specific prompts require manual engineering, while Context Tuning does not. While well-engineered natural-language prompts could yield similar improvements, such prompt design requires significant manual, task-specific effort. In contrast, Context Tuning requires no manual engineering: across all 27 BBH tasks, a single hyperparameter setting is used, with Leave-One-Out Masking and Token Dropout helping to avoid overfitting. Hence CT provides automated, task-agnostic improvements where manual prompting requires substantial effort.

Overall, Context Tuning achieves performance well beyond ICL and traditional finetuning, suggesting test-time optimization is a valuable complement to ICL. We thank the reviewer again for the thoughtful, broad questions.

# Final Decision by Area Chair

Summary:
The paper aims at improving the performance of few-shot adaptation of LLM without fine-tuning. Their methods is built upon LLLM's in-context learning ability and propose a method that initializes trainable vectors from the few-shot examples of a novel task, then optimizes them to solve each example.

Strengths identified by reviewers include the paper's presentation and its efficiency in performance and computational costs.

However, the paper also faces several critical weaknesses that have not been addressed in the rebuttal. All reviewers share the same concern that the proposed method that initializes the trainable prompt or prefix from demonstration embeddings, then optimizes as in standard prompt or prefix tuning, which is very close to prior methods and is incremental. The proposed method has limited generality and applicability across different tasks and applications scenarios such as math, coding, COT etc while general capability to handle multiple tasks simultaneously within a single model is the true merit of LLMs.

Given these considerations, this paper clearly fails to meet the standards of ICLR. The authors are encouraged to address the highlighted issues to strengthen their contribution to the field of few shot adaptation LLM.

Reviewer Concerns:
The authors provided detailed feedback and most of concerns have been addressed, including a) clearer clarification and comparisons to TTT; b) Interpretation of robustness to label noise; c) Missing specification details; d) Variable length or fixed size initialization; e) the need of clearer explanations of each designs' contributions.

However, two main concerns are still not fully addressed: 1) the proposed method that initializes the trainable prompt or prefix from demonstration embeddings, then optimizes as in standard prompt or prefix tuning, is very close to prior methods and is incremental; 2) the proposed method has limited generality and applicability across different tasks and applications scenarios such as math, coding, COT etc.
